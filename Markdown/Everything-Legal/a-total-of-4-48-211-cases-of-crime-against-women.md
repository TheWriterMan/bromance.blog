This article sheds light on the [report](https://www.ncrb.gov.in/crime-in-india-year-wise.html?year=2023&keyword=) published by the National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB) titled*Crime in India 2023*. While the report covers several important categories, this article will focus specifically on crimes against women.

## A total of 4,48,211 cases of crime against women

That we know of.

That were reported.

Even this known number paints a daunting picture of women’s safety in the 21st century. And when one considers the countless cases that go undetected and unreported, it is difficult not to feel anger and despair.

In an age of advanced technology and expanding institutional resources, the question persists:*why do these crimes continue to occur? Where are we failing? Are existing laws insufficient, or is the problem rooted in their implementation?*It is also important to note that this number continues to rise, showing an increase of**0.7% over 2022**(4,45,256 cases).

## Indian men

![](https://res.cloudinary.com/dtperak4e/image/upload/bromance-blog/medium-b8573f2678d4)*Post on reddit*On Reddit and countless other platforms, one often comes across the phrase:*“Not all men, but always a man.”*It hits hard. And it lingers.

In today’s global discourse, it is not uncommon to see Indian men stereotyped as being obsessed with sex or the idea of sex. This perception, uncomfortable as it is, demands examination rather than dismissal. The question is not whether such narratives exist, but*why*they exist. What factors feed into them?

Most people are familiar with the infamous Nirbhaya case and the [juvenile offender](https://www.voanews.com/a/juvenile-convicted-in-infamous-delhi-gang-rape-case-released/3108503.html)who evaded long-term punishment. According to multiple reports, nearly**one-third of abusers are juveniles**, the majority of whom are male. These offenders are often not strangers but*individuals known to the victim,***including family friends, relatives, neighbours, or babysitters.**## Online discourse

In recent times, social media platforms such as X and Instagram have become saturated with discussions around gender imbalance, gender inequality, and related issues. However, what becomes evident upon closer observation is that much of this discourse, if not all of it, tends to gravitate toward extremes, regardless of which side of the debate one occupies.

## The cost of silence

The figures, the cases, and the conversations, both online and offline, point toward a reality that can no longer be dismissed as isolated or accidental. Crimes against women are not merely a consequence of individual wrongdoing but the outcome of systemic failures, social conditioning, and persistent institutional apathy.

- While laws exist and numbers are recorded, justice often remains delayed, diluted, or entirely denied.
- Online discourse, instead of fostering understanding or accountability, frequently descends into extremes that obscure the lived experiences of women and derail meaningful reform.
- The focus shifts from solutions to defensiveness, from prevention to blame.

If the question is whether stricter laws are required, the more uncomfortable answer may be that enforcement, education, and accountability demand equal urgency. Without confronting patriarchal norms, improving access to reporting mechanisms, and ensuring swift and effective implementation of existing laws, statistics will continue to rise, and outrage will remain cyclical.

This is not a problem that can be solved through numbers alone or silenced through online arguments. It requires sustained societal introspection, legal responsibility, and the willingness to listen to women beyond moments of public outrage.**Until then, safety will remain conditional, justice selective, and the burden of survival unfairly placed on women.**## Resources
