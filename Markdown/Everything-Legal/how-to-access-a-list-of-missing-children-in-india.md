While researching an article, I needed access to an online database listing missing children in India. What initially appeared straightforward turned into a deeper exploration of multiple government portals and their current functionality.

There is one called [National Centre for Missing Children](http://www.missingindiankids.com/) which offers multiple services, however, this too seems to be not updated since 2010.

Then I came across another portal [Track the Missing Child](http://www.trackthemissingchild.gov.in/trackchild/index.php) by the Ministry of Women and Child Development, this too seemed to be unoperational.

Finally, I came across Mission Vatsalya portal which promotes to offer a vast list of services including providing [“a unified Digital Platform for various MIS related to children in difficult circumstances which include missing, orphaned, abandoned, and surrendered children.”](https://missionvatsalya.wcd.gov.in/public/pdf/children-related-law/vatsalyaguideline.pdf)

### The TrackChild / National Tracking System

One of the primary government platforms is the National Tracking System for Missing & Vulnerable Children (TrackChild). It was originally developed to maintain data on missing children and monitor those found and placed in Child Care Institutions.

However, the TrackChild portal now indicates that its services are being migrated to the newer unified Mission Vatsalya platform. 
 In fact, official notices state that these services have already been shifted to the Mission Vatsalya portal.

This means that while TrackChild historically functioned as a live database, it is no longer intended to be accessed as an independent public resource.

Another important limitation is that TrackChild has primarily been designed for use by law-enforcement and authorised stakeholders rather than the general public.

## Mission Vatsalya Portal

![](https://res.cloudinary.com/dtperak4e/image/upload/bromance-blog/medium-b8fb88c76c9c)

The Mission Vatsalya portal is the Government of India’s newer integrated platform intended to consolidate multiple child-protection systems.

A revamped version launched in 2025 merged earlier systems such as TrackChild and Khoya-Paya into a single interface for better coordination among government agencies and child-protection institutions.

The portal is designed to function as a unified digital platform covering children in difficult circumstances, including missing, orphaned, abandoned, and surrendered children.

However, much of the platform’s functionality appears restricted to authorised stakeholders or requires login credentials, meaning that a publicly accessible, compiled list of missing children is not readily visible to ordinary users.

## Khoya-Paya Portal

![](https://res.cloudinary.com/dtperak4e/image/upload/bromance-blog/medium-62bbb2a96bad)

![](https://res.cloudinary.com/dtperak4e/image/upload/bromance-blog/medium-582b53960954)

The Khoya-Paya portal was created as a citizen-centric platform where parents or members of the public can report a missing child or share information about a sighting.

It also allowed users to search for missing children and participate in reporting.

Yet, with the integration of systems under Mission Vatsalya, its independent role has largely been absorbed into the newer unified structure.

## So, Is There a Publicly Accessible “List” of Missing Children?

Based on current publicly available information:

- There**is**a national digital tracking infrastructure for missing children in India.
- However, the primary databases are now**integrated, restricted, or stakeholder-oriented**rather than openly browsable.
- Citizens can report cases or provide information through systems such as Khoya-Paya or helplines, but a**single, updated, publicly accessible compiled list**is not easily available without appropriate credentials or institutional access.

## Final Observation

India does have a layered digital ecosystem designed to track, monitor, and reunite missing children with their families. The consolidation under Mission Vatsalya suggests an attempt to improve coordination and real-time response.

However, from a citizen-research perspective, access to a comprehensive and updated public database remains limited. For now, individuals seeking information are more**likely to find reporting mechanisms rather than an open national list.**
