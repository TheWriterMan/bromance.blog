In 2022, I wrote an article titled [“Mental Illnesses: Inside the Mind of Infamous Criminals,”](https://advamishasingh.medium.com/mental-illnesses-inside-the-mind-of-infamous-criminals-5fe8a8fd27be) which explored the complex relationship between mental health and criminal behavior. The piece delved into the psychological profiles of notorious criminals like Jeffrey Dahmer and John Wayne Gacy, shedding light on how mental illnesses can sometimes lead to horrific acts. Building on that foundation, this article aims to examine how different countries around the world handle cases involving mentally ill offenders, with a particular focus on the insanity defense.

The concept of the insanity defense is rooted in the fundamental principle that individuals should not be held criminally responsible for their actions if they were unable to distinguish right from wrong due to severe mental illness. However, the application of this principle varies significantly across different legal systems and cultures.

## United States: A Fragmented Approach

The insanity defense has a long and contentious history in the United States. Interestingly, the defense was**first successfully used by U.S. Congressman Daniel Sickles of New York in 1859**after he had killed his wife’s lover, Philip Barton Key II. Sickles’ acquittal set a precedent that would shape the use of the insanity defense in American courts for years to come.

However, not all attempts to use this defense have been successful. A notable example is the case of Charles J. Guiteau, who assassinated President James A. Garfield in 1881. Despite Guiteau’s erratic behavior and claims of divine inspiration, his plea of temporary insanity was rejected, and he was found guilty and executed.

![](https://res.cloudinary.com/dtperak4e/image/upload/bromance-blog/medium-58c655a63b5e)*Source: https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2016/09/10/493427443/the-man-who-tried-to-kill-reagan-walks-free-with-conditions*In more recent history, the insanity defense gained widespread attention following the assassination attempt on President Ronald Reagan in 1981. John Hinckley Jr., the perpetrator, was found not guilty by reason of insanity, sparking a national debate on the use of this defense.

Hinckley’s case is particularly intriguing. Obsessed with actress Jodie Foster and the film “Taxi Driver,”**Hinckley developed erotomania**,**a****delusional disorder where one believes a person of higher status is in love with them.**> His psychiatrist testified, “He was not operating in the real world. He was operating in his own delusional world.”

The Hinckley verdict led to significant reforms in many states, making the insanity defense more difficult to prove. Today, the burden of proof varies by state, with some requiring the defense to prove insanity, while others require the prosecution to prove sanity.**Interestingly, four U.S. states — Kansas, Montana, Idaho, and Utah — have abolished the insanity defense altogether,**opting instead for a “guilty but mentally ill” verdict. This approach acknowledges the defendant’s mental illness while still holding them criminally responsible.

These historical cases and subsequent reforms highlight the ongoing struggle in the U.S. legal system to balance justice, public safety, and mental health considerations. The evolution of the insanity defense in America reflects changing societal attitudes towards mental illness and criminal responsibility.

## United Kingdom: The M’Naghten Rules

![](https://res.cloudinary.com/dtperak4e/image/upload/bromance-blog/medium-ac9de89f429a)*Source: Wikipedia*The United Kingdom follows the M’Naghten Rules, established in 1843 following Daniel M’Naghten’s acquittal for the murder of Edward Drummond, the private secretary to Prime Minister Robert Peel. M’Naghten, suffering from paranoid delusions, believed he was being persecuted by the Tories and mistook Drummond for Peel.

Under the M’Naghten Rules, the defense must prove that at the time of the offense, the defendant was laboring under such a defect of reason, from disease of the mind, as not to know the nature and quality of the act they were doing, or if they did know it, that they did not know what they were doing was wrong.

A notable case is that of Peter Sutcliffe, known as the**“Yorkshire Ripper,”**who claimed he heard voices telling him to kill prostitutes. Despite his claims, Sutcliffe was found guilty of murder. The court determined that while he may have been suffering from schizophrenia,**he was aware that his actions were legally wrong.**## Japan: A Focus on Treatment

Japan’s approach to the insanity defense is markedly different from Western countries. The Japanese system places**a strong emphasis on treatment rather than punishment for mentally ill offenders.**In 2001, Japan introduced the Medical Treatment and Supervision Act, which allows courts to order hospitalization and treatment for offenders found not guilty by reason of insanity or with diminished capacity. This system aims to provide appropriate medical care while also protecting public safety.

A high-profile case that illustrates this approach is that of**Tomohiro Kato, who killed seven people in a vehicular and stabbing attack in Tokyo’s Akihabara district in 2008.**While Kato was ultimately found criminally responsible and sentenced to death, the case sparked a national conversation about mental health and criminal justice.

## India: Evolving Perspectives

In India, the insanity defense is covered under**Section 84 of the Indian Penal Code (Section 22 of BNS)**, which states that nothing is an offense which is done by a person who, at the time of doing it, by reason of unsoundness of mind, is incapable of knowing the nature of the act, or that he is doing what is either wrong or contrary to law.

However, the application of this law has been evolving. In the case of Surendra Mishra v. State of Jharkhand (2011), the Supreme Court of India emphasized that the insanity defense should be applied judiciously, stating,:

> “The mere fact that the accused is conceited, odd, irascible and his brain is not quite all right, or that the physical and mental ailments from which he suffered had rendered his intellect weak and affected his emotions or indulges in certain unusual acts, or had fits of insanity at short intervals or that he was subject to epileptic fits and there was abnormal behavior or the behavior is queer are not sufficient to attract the application of Section 84 of IPC.”

## Netherlands: A Unique Gradation System

The Netherlands employs a unique system that recognizes varying degrees of criminal responsibility based on the severity of mental illness.**Dutch law allows for five levels of diminished responsibility:**slightly diminished, diminished, considerably diminished, severely diminished, and complete absence of responsibility.**This nuanced approach allows for more tailored sentencing and treatment options.**For instance, in the case of Volkert van der Graaf, who assassinated politician Pim Fortuyn in 2002, the court found him to have “slightly diminished” responsibility due to narcissistic personality disorder. This finding influenced his sentence but did not exculpate him entirely.

## Australia: The Civil Commitment Alternative

Australia’s approach to the insanity defense varies by state, but a common feature is the use of civil commitment for those found not guilty by reason of mental illness. This system allows for indefinite detention in a psychiatric facility, with regular reviews to assess the individual’s mental state and risk to the community.

The case of Martin Bryant, responsible for the Port Arthur massacre in 1996, highlights the complexities of this system. Despite exhibiting signs of mental illness, Bryant was found fit to stand trial and criminally responsible for his actions. This case led to debates about the adequacy of mental health assessments in high-profile criminal cases.

## Global Challenges and Controversies

While the insanity defense exists in various forms across the globe, its application remains controversial and challenging. Some common issues include:

1. Difficulty in assessing mental state at the time of the offense
2. Potential for abuse of the defense
3. Public perception and the stigma associated with mental illness
4. Balancing public safety with the rights and treatment needs of mentally ill offenders

The case of Anders Breivik in Norway exemplifies these challenges.**Breivik, who killed 77 people in a 2011 terror attack,**was initially diagnosed with paranoid schizophrenia. However, a second evaluation found him sane, leading to a heated debate about**the role of psychiatry in criminal justice.**## Conclusion

The global landscape of the insanity defense reveals a complex interplay between legal traditions, cultural attitudes towards mental illness, and evolving understandings of psychology and neuroscience. While approaches vary significantly from country to country, there is a growing recognition of the need to balance public safety with humane treatment of mentally ill offenders.

As our understanding of mental health continues to advance, it’s likely that legal systems worldwide will continue to grapple with these issues, potentially leading to more nuanced and effective approaches to handling cases involving mentally ill criminals.

The challenge moving forward will be to develop systems that can accurately assess criminal responsibility, provide appropriate treatment, and protect public safety, all while respecting the rights of those with mental illnesses. As we’ve seen from the diverse approaches around the world, there’s no one-size-fits-all solution, but by learning from each other’s experiences, we can work towards more just and effective systems.
