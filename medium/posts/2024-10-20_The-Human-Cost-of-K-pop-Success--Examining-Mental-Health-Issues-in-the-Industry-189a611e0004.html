The Human Cost of K-pop Success: Examining Mental Health Issues in the Industry

By Advocate Amisha Singh

In my recent article, “The Legal Labyrinth of K-pop: Exploring the Complex World of Idol Contracts in South Korea,” I delved into the intricate and often controversial contractual landscape that governs the K-pop industry. The piece attracted attention, and a reader’s comment particularly resonated with me. They asked, “Don’t you think the contract or all this system is so messed up in Korea?”

This question prompted me to dig deeper, beyond the legal intricacies, into the human impact of these systemic issues. While contracts and legal frameworks form the skeleton of the K-pop industry, it’s the mental and emotional well-being of the idols themselves that truly flesh out the picture of what it means to be a K-pop star.

In the dazzling world of K-pop, where perfectly choreographed performances and chart-topping hits dominate global music scenes, there lies a darker, often unexplored undercurrent. Behind the glitz and glamour, many K-pop idols grapple with severe mental health challenges, raising critical questions about the human cost of success in this high-pressure industry.

This article aims to peel back the layers of the K-pop phenomenon, examining how the very system that produces global superstars can also be a breeding ground for mental health issues. From the grueling training periods to the relentless public scrutiny, we’ll explore the various factors that contribute to the mental health crisis in K-pop and consider potential paths toward a healthier, more sustainable industry.

The Pressure Cooker of Perfection

From a young age, aspiring K-pop idols enter a rigorous training system. Some begin as young as 11 or 12, sacrificing their childhood and teenage years for the dream of stardom. The pressure to excel is immense, with trainees often practicing for up to 16 hours a day, seven days a week.

Take the case of Jonghyun, a member of the popular group SHINee, who tragically took his own life in 2017. In his final message, he wrote about the overwhelming pressure he faced: “I’m broken from the inside… The depression that has slowly eaten away at me has finally consumed me, and I couldn’t beat it.”

This incident shed light on the mental toll of the K-pop lifestyle, prompting industry-wide discussions about mental health. However, years later, many argue that substantial changes are yet to be seen.

The Weight of Public Scrutiny

Once debuted, idols face relentless public scrutiny. Every action, both on and off stage, is meticulously analyzed by fans and critics alike. Social media, while a powerful tool for fan engagement, can also become a breeding ground for cyberbullying and excessive criticism.

Sulli, formerly of the girl group f(x), was a victim of such intense online harassment. Known for her outspoken nature and unconventional behavior by K-pop standards, she faced years of malicious comments. In 2019, she tragically ended her life, highlighting the devastating impact of cyberbullying on mental health.

The “Perfect” Image and Body Standards

K-pop idols are expected to maintain an unrealistic standard of physical perfection. Strict diets and exhaustive exercise regimens are common, often leading to eating disorders and body dysmorphia.

IU, one of Korea’s most successful solo artists, has been open about her struggles with bulimia and eating disorders. In an interview, she revealed, “There was a time when I couldn’t eat a single meal for three weeks and survived only on water.” Her story is not unique in the industry, where extreme dieting is often normalized and even praised.

Lack of Privacy and Personal Life

The demanding nature of idol life leaves little room for personal time or relationships. Many companies impose “no-dating” clauses in contracts, effectively controlling idols’ personal lives.

This lack of normalcy can lead to feelings of isolation and loneliness. As Taeyeon of Girls’ Generation once shared, “Not being able to date publicly is a given in this field. But sometimes, I want to date like everyone else.”

Overwork and Exhaustion

The K-pop industry is known for its grueling schedules. Idols often work around the clock, participating in performances, variety shows, fan meetings, and more. This constant activity leaves little time for rest and recovery.

In 2019, Mina of TWICE took a hiatus due to anxiety and panic disorders, likely exacerbated by the group’s intense schedule. TWICE had been one of the busiest groups in K-pop, with barely any breaks between comebacks and world tours.

The Stigma of Mental Health in Korean Society

Compounding these issues is the broader societal stigma surrounding mental health in South Korea. Seeking help for mental health concerns is often seen as a sign of weakness, discouraging many idols from openly discussing their struggles or seeking professional help.

BTS’s Suga, who has been vocal about his experiences with depression and social anxiety, stated in an interview, “We’re in a position where we can’t make mistakes, but we’re still human. We struggle with mental health issues just like everyone else.”

Steps Towards Change

While the situation remains challenging, there are signs of positive change in the industry:

1. Mental Health Awareness: Some companies are starting to prioritize mental health. For instance, Big Hit Entertainment (now HYBE) has implemented regular counseling sessions for their artists.

2. Reduced Schedules: Some groups are being given more downtime between comebacks and tours. BLACKPINK, for example, has longer breaks between promotions compared to many other groups.

3. Open Discussions: More idols are speaking out about mental health. EXO’s Chen has been open about his experiences with panic disorder, helping to normalize these conversations.

4. Legislative Changes: The Korean government has started to take notice. In 2019, they implemented new regulations to protect the rights of underage entertainers, including mandatory breaks and education requirements.

The Road Ahead

Despite these positive steps, there’s still a long way to go. The K-pop industry needs to address several key areas:

1. Comprehensive Mental Health Support: Regular mental health check-ups and readily available counseling services should be industry standard.

2. Work-Life Balance: Implementing reasonable work hours and ensuring adequate rest periods between schedules is crucial.

3. Body Positivity: Promoting healthier body images and discouraging extreme dieting practices is essential for both physical and mental well-being.

4. Education and Awareness: Both idols and the public need better education about mental health to reduce stigma and encourage seeking help when needed.

5. Cyberbullying Prevention: Stricter measures against online harassment and better support systems for idols facing cyberbullying are necessary.

Conclusion

The mental health crisis in the K-pop industry is a complex issue rooted in cultural expectations, industry practices, and societal pressures. While the glittering facade of K-pop continues to captivate global audiences, it’s crucial to recognize and address the human cost behind this success.

As fans, industry professionals, and observers, we have a responsibility to advocate for better practices and support systems. The true measure of K-pop’s success should not just be in album sales or YouTube views, but in the well-being and happiness of the artists who bring this music to life.

The path to change may be long, but it’s a necessary journey. As Suga of BTS poignantly said, “We’re all in different parts of the world, in different environments and circumstances, but I hope we can all say, ‘It’s okay to not be okay.’” It’s time for the K-pop industry to truly embrace this sentiment and prioritize the mental health of its stars.
