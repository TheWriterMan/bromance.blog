My thoughts on self-help books

By ASRwritings

Song to listen to while reading: Aerosmith — Dream On

My favourite genre to read has always been fiction.

I loved the way it held my hand and quietly led me out of my own life for a while. As a child, that escape felt earned, like a small luxury I was allowed to keep just for myself.

Growing up, though, I stopped wanting to run away. I wanted to understand where I was standing. And to do that, I went looking for a guide.

Being a reader, I did what I knew best. I turned to books.

Naturally, I picked self-help.

After reading my fair share of the genre, here are my two cents on it.

Did it help?

While reading, a thought kept circling back to me.
 I already know this.
 I already feel this.

By the time I closed the book, it felt less like I had learned something new and more like I had been reminded of things I already carried within me. When this happened again and again, with almost every book, the excitement slowly thinned out.

What remained was a strange emptiness. No satisfaction. Just the quiet awareness of time spent and nothing to hold in my hands afterward.

I don’t think self-help books work in isolation. They seem to need something else to lean on. Conversations. People. A family, a friend, or simply someone willing to listen when the pages fall silent.

They didn’t transform me or turn my life into something shinier. But they gave me language. Words for feelings I had been carrying without names. They helped me understand that sometimes nothing is wrong with me.

A book, by itself, might not make me a better person. But it offers a kind of emotional support that isn’t always available elsewhere. A quiet reassurance that I’m not alone. That I’m not strange or broken for feeling the way I do.
